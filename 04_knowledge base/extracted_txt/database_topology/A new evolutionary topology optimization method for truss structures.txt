ABSTRACT
This paper presents a new topology optimization method and a comprehensive workflow for the practical design 
of truss structures. It begins with discussing practical design requirements for truss topology optimization and 
then introduces a technique for creating arbitrarily shaped ground structures suitable for complex geometries. To 
address limitations in current truss optimization methods, we propose a dual-material truss-bidirectional 
evolutionary structural optimization (DMT-BESO) method. This approach utilizes two materials that differ 
significantly in tensile and compressive allowable stresses and moduli of elasticity. The DMT-BESO method in -
tegrates the minimum energy principle with the full-stress design criterion, using bar cross-sectional areas as 
design variables to achieve simultaneous topology and size optimization. By considering stress constraints, this 
method ensures compliance with industry standards, enhancing both safety and material utilization. Addition -
ally, a structural complexity control strategy is proposed to generate a near-optimal truss design and simplify the 
optimized design while maintaining efficiency, making it more suitable for practical applications. The effec -
tiveness of the DMT-BESO method and its complexity control strategy is validated through numerical examples 
and the design of an arch bridge of composite materials.
1. Introduction
Topology optimization (TO) is a vital performance-based design 
technique aimed at optimizing the distribution of materials within a 
prescribed design domain to maximize the performance of the resultant 
structure. This technique has gained significant attention in both 
continuous and discrete design domains, leading to rapid methodolog -
ical advancements over the recent decades. The most popular and well- 
established methods of continuum-based TO, widely applied across 
various disciplines, can be broadly categorized into four types: (1) ho -
mogenization methods based on micro-perforated composite materials 
[1] ; (2) material density distribution methods, epitomized by the solid 
isotropic material with penalization (SIMP) method [2 – 4] ; (3) evolu -
tionary methods, both the evolutionary structural optimization (ESO) 
method [5,6] and bi-directional ESO (BESO) method [7] ; and (4) 
boundary variation methods, such as the level set-based method [8,9]
and the phase field method [10] . Recent trends in TO focus on 
improving computational efficiency [11] and integrating multi-physics 
fields [12] , multi-scale [13] , and multi-objective [14] considerations 
to address real-world engineering challenges. Considering uncertainties 
in material properties and loading conditions, robust TO [15,16] is 
essential for practical applications, enhancing the reliability and per -
formance of optimized structures. Continuous improvements in algo -
rithms have made TO technology a powerful tool for designers to 
explore lightweight, innovative, and high-performance structures in the 
conceptual design process [17 – 22] .
Undoubtedly, designers using continuum-based TO techniques can 
obtain conceptual solutions with unique forms and superior structural 
performance in the architectural and civil engineering fields [23 – 25] . 
However, despite extensive and in-depth academic research on TO al -
gorithms, their application in practical projects remains limited. Con -
tinuum TO often generates irregular shapes that are difficult to use in

In contrast, truss structures, known for their excellent stiffness-to- 
weight ratios, modularity, material efficiency, and load capacity, are 
widely used in engineering due to their simplicity and ease of con -
struction. Trusses consist of straight members connected by hinges, 
making them ideal for optimization. However, continuum-based TO 
approaches are unsuitable for hinge-connected truss structures. There -
fore, investigating TO algorithms specifically for truss structures is 
crucial for practical design applications.
In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Maxwell [26] and Cilly [27]
proposed the basic idea of TO for truss structures. Later, Michell [28]
proposed the Michell truss model with minimum volume under stress 
constraints. In the stress-constrained optimization model, he proved that 
the bars in the structure with the minimum volume under the single load 
case are in a full stress state. However, despite the Michell truss being 
proved to be the optimal design under stress constraints, it is difficult to 
apply in practical engineering since it contains an infinite number of 
bars. Until Dorn et al. [29] proposed the ground structure (GS) method 
in 1964, the numerical implementation of truss TO became feasible and 
gained practical engineering design significance. Since then, numerous 
scholars have conducted extensive investigations around the GS method. 
Although this method is the most widely used for truss TO, weaknesses 
still exist, including: (1) the optimal topology is highly dependent on the 
initial GS; (2) the optimal solutions often contain too many bars and 
most of them are too thin to be used directly; and (3) the design domains 
used for theoretical studies are usually limited to regular geometric 
shapes such as rectangles, where GS nodes can be arranged in a square 
grid layout, while the design domain of complex irregular shapes should 
be considered in practical engineering design. To address these issues, a 
series of effective solutions have been proposed by Martinez et al. [30] , 
Mckeown [31] , Rule [32] , He et al. [33] , Gilbert and Tyas [34] , Wang 
and Arora [35] , Lu and Xie [36] . However, truss TO also requires 
consideration of complex structural geometries, irregular GS generation, 
and multi-material optimization in real engineering applications, which 
have been less discussed in previous studies.
In view of this, this study first focuses on the creation of truss GSs 
within arbitrary shape design domains and the workflow for achieving 
TO. Furthermore, building upon the BESO algorithm for truss structures 
proposed by Cai and Feng [37] , our new algorithm incorporates the 
differences in tensile and compressive properties of two materials. The 
remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section 2 outlines 
practical design requirements in truss TO, approaches to creating arbi -
trary GSs, and TO implementation platforms. Section 3 summarizes the 
theoretical background of the BESO method for truss structures and 
presents the dual-material truss BESO algorithm. Sections 4 and 5
demonstrate the 2D and 3D numerical examples, respectively. Finally, 
conclusions are drawn in Section 6 .
2. GS creation approach, TO implementation platform and 
workflows
2.1. Practical design requirements in truss TO
Despite the powerful capabilities of current TO techniques and the 
potential for significant applications demonstrated, practical challenges 
are commonly encountered, including poor practicability due to overly 
complex outcomes and incoherent integration with existing workflows. 
Practical design requirements for truss TO include critical issues such as 
local or global instability [38,39] that have already been addressed. 
Recent developments in truss TO show that hybrid algorithms effec -
tively handle topology, size, and shape optimization, while improving 
structural performance and efficiency. Dang et al. [40] introduced a 
single-step hybrid method that enhances computational efficiency for 
complex truss designs, while Lieu [41] presented a unified framework to 
address static, dynamic, and transient behaviors of trusses. Similarly, 
Oguz et al. [42] proposed a global optimization strategy tailored for 
additive manufacturing, emphasizing the importance of integrating 
design constraints during the TO process. Further investigations should 
focus on manufacturability, simplicity, workflow integration, material 
efficiency, and condition constraints to ensure the practical imple -
mentation of TO in real-world applications. Adhering to these principles 
guarantees the development of efficient, high-performance truss struc -
tures suitable for practical use. 
(1) Manufacturability: Optimized designs must be feasible with 
existing fabrication techniques, considering constraints such as 
member length, minimum cross-sectional areas, and specific 
manufacturing processes. Addressing manufacturability ensures 
smoother transitions from design to construction, reducing the 
need for custom fabrication.
(2) Simplicity and elegance: While aiming for optimal performance, 
the truss TO outcomes should remain simple and elegant. Overly 
complex solutions can complicate manufacturing, assembly, and 
maintenance, whereas simpler designs are typically more prac -
tical and cost-effective.
(3) Material efficiency: Incorporating materials with distinct tensile 
and compressive properties enhances the structural performance 
and sustainability. Utilizing multiple materials allows for opti -
mized designs that leverage the strengths of each material, 
resulting in high-performance, cost-effective, and sustainable 
structures.
(4) Conditional constraints: Addressing multiple constraints such as 
stresses, member cross-sectional areas, and serviceability ensures 
that truss designs meet the diverse and complex demands of real- 
world engineering applications. These constraints contribute to 
structural robustness and reliability.
(5) Integration with existing workflows: The TO process should 
integrate seamlessly with current design and engineering work -
flows, including compatibility with CAD software and other en -
gineering tools. Effective integration facilitates the adoption of 
optimized designs within broader project workflows.
2.2. Approach to creating arbitrary morphological GSs
The so-called GS method involves creating a network of trusses by 
connecting discrete nodes based on the design domain’s shape and 
boundary conditions. However, generating GS using traditional MAT -
LAB codes [43,44] , although systematic, often encounters challenges in 
terms of flexibility in handling complex geometries and practical 
application to real-world scenarios. To address this, we have developed 
a parameterized approach using Rhino and Grasshopper to create GSs.
The Rhino software, extensively used across multiple industries, 
excels in managing complex and irregular geometries, offering more 
flexibility than MATLAB-based methods. Rhino’s advanced visualization 
tools enable designers to visualize complex geometries in 3D, aiding the 
understanding of spatial relationships and potential design issues. De -
signers can effortlessly create intricate shapes and adapt GSs to fit non- 
standard design domains. The Grasshopper plug-in provides powerful 
parametric design capabilities, allowing users to quickly modify and 
iterate designs in a visual programming environment.This iterative 
process and flexibility facilitate efficient design refinement and explo -
ration of various design alternatives and optimizations. Additionally, 
Rhino integrates seamlessly with other CAD software, enhancing the 
practicality and applicability of GSs in broader design processes.
A simple and efficient approach, as shown in Fig. 1 (a), involves using 
the "Grasshopper Python Script Editor" for creating GSs. Users input 
relevant parameters in the Python code, and the Abaqus input file (.inp) 
information for the entire GS is automatically generated. For example, in 
creating a regular planar mesh GS, as shown in Fig. 1 (b), the process first 
begins with defining key parameters such as grid dimensions, grid 
spacing, and connection levels. Next, the script generates a grid of points

To obtain complex and arbitrary GSs, another method involves 
parametric modeling using Rhino and Grasshopper by defining 3D ge -

spatial control lines, enabling the 3D geometry of the GSs to be tuned

creating an arch-shaped 3D spatial GS shown in Figs. 2 (a) and (b), the

in Fig. 2 (c); followed by setting control parameters such as the number 
of nodes along the control lines and the maximum angle between con -
nections (or bars), as shown in Fig. 2 (d). This parameter-based model 
can provide multiple solutions depending on the values of each 
parameter. By defining the parametric files generated during the 
modeling process as reusable functional files, the efficiency of modeling 
the GSs can be significantly improved.
Regardless of the approach adopted, the initial GS design data is 
generated in Grasshopper. The GS input file for finite element analysis 
(FEA) is created and exported in strict accordance with the structure and 
format of the Abaqus input file (.inp).
2.3. TO implementation platform and workflows
Abaqus, a versatile and general-purpose FEA software package, is 
widely used for its robust capabilities in performing complex simula -
tions and analyses. The Abaqus scripting interface, powered by Python, 
provides comprehensive access to the software ’ s advanced FEA func -
tionalities, allowing for the automation of modeling, analysis, and post- 
processing tasks. This integration facilitates the seamless incorporation 
of custom algorithms and workflows into the Abaqus environment, 
which is particularly beneficial for iterative TO. The implementation of 
our TO process follows a systematic workflow comprising the following 
key steps: 
(1) Import initial design: The initial design, generated from the.inp 
file exported by Grasshopper, serves as the starting point for the 
optimization process.

strating its accuracy and effectiveness. For more details on the T-BESO 
method, see Ref. [37] .
3.2. Dual-materials T-BESO
Considering multiple materials with differences in tensile and 
compressive properties in truss TO is essential for optimizing material 
usage and improving cost-effectiveness. Assigning materials tailored to 
force characteristics not only enhances structural performance but also 
provides innovative and adaptable solutions. Therefore, we have further 
developed a dual-material T-BESO (DMT-BESO) method based on the T- 
BESO algorithmic framework, allowing the use of two materials with 
significant differences in allowable stress and elastic modulus for tensile 
and compressive bars, respectively.
This approach is particularly valuable in practical engineering, 
where materials like steel and concrete, with inherently different tensile 
and compressive strengths, are often used together to achieve more 
efficient and resilient structures. By incorporating materials with 
distinct properties, truss TO can minimize material consumption, reduce 
construction costs, and increase the durability of the final design, 
making it highly applicable to industries such as civil engineering and 
infrastructure development.
3.2.1. Prerequisite assumptions
In this study, we have simplified the DMT-BESO process to focus on 
overall structural performance and material distribution. Challenges 
related to interfacial regions are addressed under the following 
assumptions: 
(1) Linear elastic material behavior: Both materials are assumed to 
exhibit linear elastic behavior throughout the optimization pro -
cess. This simplification allows us to focus on structural perfor -
mance, avoiding the complexities associated with nonlinearities, 
such as plastic deformation or material failure at the interfaces.

(2) Minimal structural deformation: The structure is assumed to 
undergo minimal deformation in response to external forces. This 
small deformation assumption simplifies the analysis, ensuring 
that stresses and displacements remain within the linear elastic 
regime.
(3) Continuity at the interface: A key assumption is that the materials 
maintain complete continuity at the connection interface. 
Displacement and stress are continuous across the interface, 
ensuring fully coupled mechanical behavior. Consequently, no 
slip, segregation, or stress concentration occurs at the interface, 
and the connection behaves as though the two materials are 
perfectly bonded.
(4) Localized node connections: The connection between different 
materials is treated as a localized issue at the nodes. Specific 
interfacial behaviors, such as adhesion, bonding or local stress 
concentration, are not considered in this study. Instead, the 
connection is simplified to ensure cohesive behavior between the 
materials in the global optimization process. Addressing the 
detailed mechanics of interfacial behavior is beyond the scope of 
this study but could be explored in future research.

energy ensures that the structure achieves optimal stiffness under 
the applied loads, which is essential for mechanical efficiency and 
serviceability. Structures with minimized strain energy are 
inherently more resistant to deformation and tend to exhibit su -
perior performance, particularly under cyclic loading conditions. 
The DMT-BESO method uses strain energy minimization as the 
objective function by combining the energy principle with the 
full-stress design criterion. When the full stress condition is met, 
each member in the truss is fully utilized to its capacity, effec -
tively leading to an optimal material distribution.

3.2.8. Procedure
The evolutionary iteration process of the DMT-BESO method in -
volves the following steps: 
(1) Establish the GS, define loads, boundary conditions, and initialize 
design variables.
(2) Perform FEA to calculate normal stresses for each element.
(3) Calculate the variations of tensile and compressive material 
properties during the optimization process based on the iteration 
number.
(4) Compute element sensitivities and sensitivity thresholds.
(5) Assign tensile material properties to bars in tension and 
compressive material properties to bars in compression.
(6) Compare element sensitivities with thresholds and adjust design 
variables accordingly.
(7) Check for convergence using the specified criterion. If conver -
gence is not achieved, repeat the process from step (2).
Fig. 6 shows the DMT-BESO method flowchart. The algorithm is 
programmed in Python and integrated with the Abaqus software that is 
used as a FEA solver.
3.3. Structural complexity control strategy
In the context of truss TO, controlling structural complexity is crucial 
for achieving a practical and manufacturable design. Overly complex 
truss structures are difficult to fabricate, assemble, and maintain, lead -
ing to higher costs. Minimizing unnecessary complexity by reducing the 
number of thin or non-essential bars contributes to the design of simple 
and elegant structures while preserving load-carrying capacity and 
robustness.

approach for generating the near-optimal truss designs rather than 
strictly theoretical optimal solutions. Therefore, we propose a simple

5. A practical application
In this section, we applied the DMT-BESO method for the structural 
optimization of a single-span through arch bridge, focusing on opti -
mizing the hanger arrangement for structural performance. The bridge, 
designed with a main span of 120 m and a width of 10 m, features a 
basket handle arch that slopes inward to enhance both stability and 
aesthetics. The composite structure incorporates ultra-high- 
performance concrete (UHPC) for its excellent compressive strength 
and high-strength steel for its superior tensile properties, as detailed in 
Table 4 . The modulus of elasticity and allowable stresses for these ma -
terials were referenced from [53,54] .
For structural optimization of this bridge, the dominant load condi -
tions considered were dead and vehicle loads, which were converted 
into concentrated loads imposed on the tie beam nodes. Besides, to 
simplify the calculations while providing safe and reliable results, the 
vehicle static load approximation and appropriate impact coefficients 
were used to capture the effects of dynamic loads. Since bridge struc -
tures experience variable loads during service, leading to different stress 
distributions and potential failure modes, optimizing for a single load 
condition can result in designs that fail under different or combined 
loads. Therefore, in the optimization process, in addition to applying 
structural complexity control to simplify the structure, we employed 
multiple load cases, i.e., superimposing the dead load and the vehicle 
moving load into five major load cases in order to simulate the vehicle 
travelling across the bridge. The TO processes converge after 419 iter -
ations, with total elapsed time of 216 min.
The arch-shaped 3D spatial GS shown in Fig. 14 (a) consists of 814 
bars and was generated via the second method introduced in Section 2.2 . 
As depicted in Fig. 14 (b), the nodes are distributed solely on the top and 
bottom edges of the GS, while the bars intersect without forming addi -
tional intersections. The initial cross-sectional area of each bar, along 
with the minimum and maximum values of the cross-sectional area 
constraints, aligns with those described in Section 4.1 . Figs. 14 (c) to (g) 
illustrate the GS with boundary conditions under the five major load 
cases and corresponding optimized designs. The stress color maps 
indicate that the stress levels of all bars remain within acceptable limits, 
ensuring the bridge can safely withstand the applied loads while effi -
ciently using materials. The optimized structure conforms to the 
fundamental force characteristics of a through tie-arch bridge, with 
UHPC allocated to the arch ribs and high-strength steel to the tie-beams 
and hangers, ensuring each component is suited to its respective force 
regime.
By exporting the optimized structure from Abaqus in * .odb file 
format and incorporating the CAD model into the 3D environment, the 
design can be further analyzed and evaluated. The rendering shown in 
Fig. 15 highlights the elegance and practicality of this bridge, demon -
strating the seamless integration of the novel structural optimization 
techniques with practical design aesthetics.

method, the dual-material truss BESO (DMT-BESO), which optimizes 
truss structures by simultaneously using two materials with distinct 
tensile and compressive properties. The following conclusions can be 
drawn: 
(1) Practical design considerations for truss topology optimization 
encompass manufacturability, simplicity, material efficiency, 
specific constraints, and smooth integration into existing work -
flows. Achieving a balance between theoretical optimization and 
practical implementation requires a comprehensive approach.
(2) A new approach for generating arbitrarily shaped ground struc -
tures has been developed, utilizing the parametric capabilities of 
Grasshopper and Rhino. The integration of Abaqus, Grasshopper, 
and Python facilitates handling complex geometries and solving 
practical engineering problems.
(3) By employing the minimum energy principle and full-stress 
design criterion, the DMT-BESO method achieves topology and 
size optimization simultaneously, while stress constraints ensure 
compliance with industry standards, promoting both safety and 
material efficiency.
(4) The effectiveness of the DMT-BESO method along with its 
structural complexity control strategy, has been validated 
through multiple numerical examples, including the design of a 
composite material arch bridge, demonstrating its capacity to 
deliver both efficient and practical truss structures.
The DMT-BESO method offers a powerful tool for practical truss 
design, combining theoretical foundations with practical design con -
siderations. Future in-depth research could focus on incorporate Euler 
buckling constraints for compressive members in the optimization pro -
cess, enhancing computational efficiency and extending the application 
to larger, more complex structural systems.
